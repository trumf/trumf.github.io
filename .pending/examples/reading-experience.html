The Principles of Readable Typography

Typography plays a crucial role in digital design, affecting not just aesthetics but also readability, accessibility, and user engagement. This article explores the science-backed principles that make text more readable.

The Science of Reading

Reading is a complex cognitive process that involves recognizing patterns of letters, processing their meaning, and connecting them to existing knowledge. Our eyes don't move continuously along lines of text; instead, they make quick movements called saccades, with brief stops called fixations where we actually process information.

Good typography works with these natural reading patterns rather than against them. It reduces cognitive load and eye strain, allowing readers to focus on content rather than struggling with its presentation.

Typography is what language looks like.

Ellen Lupton, Thinking with Type

Optimal Line Length

One of the most critical factors in readability is line length. Lines that are too long force readers to move their eyes too far, making it difficult to find the beginning of the next line. Lines that are too short break the reading rhythm with too many line breaks.

Research shows that the ideal line length is between 50-75 characters (including spaces) per line. This translates to approximately 65 characters per line for most body text, which we've implemented using the ch unit in CSS:

p {
  max-width: 65ch; /* Limits width to approximately 65 characters */
}

Different types of content may benefit from different line lengths:

  • Focused reading (articles, essays): 65ch
  • General content (mixed text and images): 75ch
  • Technical content (code samples, tables): 90ch

Line Height and Spacing

Line height (leading) affects how easily readers can track from the end of one line to the beginning of the next. Text with inadequate line height feels cramped and difficult to follow.

For body text, a line height of 1.5 to 1.6 times the font size provides good readability. Headings typically need less line height (1.2 to 1.3) due to their larger font size. We've implemented this with CSS variables:

:root {
  --leading-normal: 1.6; /* For body text */
  --leading-tight: 1.2;  /* For headings */
}

Paragraph Spacing

Proper spacing between paragraphs helps readers understand the content structure. The space between paragraphs should be larger than the space between lines within a paragraph, creating a clear visual distinction.

This spacing rhythm creates a comfortable reading flow, allowing the eye to naturally follow the content structure. We've implemented a consistent paragraph spacing with:

p {
  margin-bottom: var(--paragraph-spacing);
}

Typography Hierarchy

A clear typographic hierarchy guides readers through content, helping them understand its structure and importance. Our system uses a combination of size, weight, and spacing to create distinct levels:

Heading Levels

Each heading level has distinct characteristics that reflect its importance in the content hierarchy:

  • H1: Page titles, article headlines (largest size, boldest weight)
  • H2: Major section headings (medium-large size, medium weight)
  • H3: Subsection headings (medium size, medium weight)
  • H4: Minor section headings (small-medium size, medium weight)
  • H5: Specialized headings (base size, uppercase, medium weight)

Letter Spacing

Letter spacing (tracking) adjustments fine-tune readability. Generally:

  • Large headings benefit from slightly tighter letter spacing
  • Body text works well with normal letter spacing
  • Small text and all-caps text need slightly wider letter spacing for legibility

Responsive Typography

As screens shrink, typography must adapt. Our system adjusts font sizes, line heights, and spacing at different breakpoints to maintain readability across devices.

On mobile devices, we make these adjustments:

  • Slightly smaller font sizes
  • Reduced line height (1.5 instead of 1.6)
  • Full-width text containers
  • Reduced paragraph spacing
